Inspiration

This project was born out of our collective fascination with missing person cases. Each of us indulged our curiosity through various mediums—YouTube, podcasts, and books. During our first semester at Dartmouth, we bonded over evenings spent immersed in The Missing Enigma YouTube channel. When the chance to choose a project presented itself, it was only natural for us to delve into the realm of missing persons data.

Data Proposal -> Introduction

In the United States, there are currently more than 20,000 active missing persons cases [3]. Some of these individuals are found alive, but many have fates that remain unknown. The large number of cases per year raise fundamental questions about broader societal, criminological, and other risk factors contributing to their disappearance [1]. Given the documented link between drug use and criminality [2], we are motivated to conduct this case study. We seek to observe patterns and correlations that may exist between drug usable, crime rates, and incidence of missing persons in certain areas. Our research relies on data from NamUS, a comprehensive missing persons in the US database, as its foundation. Our objective is to explore underlying determinants influencing the occurrence of missing persons cases, while working to develop effective strategies for their prevention and investigation.

Data Discription

We have compiled files from three sources to aid in the analysis for this case study. Firstly, the NamUS dataset, as previously mentioned, has been filtered to encompass missing persons data spanning the years 2011 to 2018 [3]. This dataset comprises information on 9,828 individuals, including their case number, name, current age, age at the time of disappearance, last known location, and ethnicity. The second source is data from the Federal Bureau of Investigation, which includes crime statistics by state, spanning the years 1960 to 2018 [4]. This dataset provides insights into the state's population and rates of various criminal activities, such as murder, burglary, arson, robbery, and assault, expressed as the number of reported offenses per 100,000 individuals in the population. By incorporating this data into our study, we aim to explore potential correlations between the number of missing persons and elevated crime rates in specific states. Our primary focus will be on murder, but we will also make observations regarding other criminal activities. Our third source is the National Survey on Drug Use and Health, which provides us with a shapefile containing outcome variables, including alcohol use, mental illness, serious mental illness, and various illicit drug usage patterns [5]. This data will enable us to investigate potential connections between drug use and missing persons cases, as well as other relevant factors.
